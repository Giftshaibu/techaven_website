How to Compare Phones Before Buying

Better comparison starts with your real use case — not spec sheet numbers.

Back to blog
By Techaven Editorial Team Published 30 January 2026 Updated 2 May 2026

Comparison shopping for phones is easy to do badly. Opening five browser tabs, skimming spec sheets, and picking the highest numbers produces purchases people regret. Higher megapixels do not mean better photos. More cores do not always mean faster real-world performance. And a number that looks impressive on paper can mean nothing to your actual daily use in Lilongwe, Blantyre, or anywhere else in Malawi.

This guide gives you a framework for comparison that is grounded in real use, not marketing figures. Use it alongside Techaven's marketplace to shortlist options from verified sellers before deciding.

1. Start With Your Use Case, Not the Specs

Before opening a single comparison, list what you actually do on your phone for an entire week. Be honest. For most Malawians, the list includes: voice calls, WhatsApp and Messenger, Airtel Money or TNM Mpamba transactions, social media (Facebook, TikTok, Instagram), basic photography, music, and occasional mobile data browsing. That list tells you exactly which specs actually matter for you.

For that workload — which covers the majority of Malawian smartphone users — RAM above 6GB, flagship cameras, and top-tier processors provide zero additional value. The phones you should be comparing are budget-to-mid-range Android devices, not flagships. Comparing a Samsung A35 with a Samsung A55 is useful. Comparing either with a Samsung S24 Ultra is not — they are in different categories for different users.

2. Compare Within the Same Price Tier

Cross-tier comparisons do not produce useful insights for buying decisions. If your budget is MWK 200,000 to MWK 300,000, comparing phones within that range tells you what the best option is at that price. Comparing options in that range to a MWK 600,000 phone just tells you the expensive phone is better — which you already knew, and which does not help you decide.

The price tiers that matter in Malawi's 2026 market: Under MWK 100k (entry-level), MWK 100k–250k (budget mid-range), MWK 250k–500k (mid-range), and MWK 500k+ (upper/flagship). Decide your tier first, then compare within it.

3. Processor Tiers Simply Explained

Processor marketing is confusing by design. Here is a simplified breakdown relevant to phones available in Malawi:

  • Budget chips (MediaTek Helio G-series, Unisoc): Handle everyday tasks, WhatsApp, social media, and mobile money fine. Slow under sustained load. Adequate for MWK 80k–150k phones.
  • Mid-range chips (MediaTek Dimensity 700/800/1000, Snapdragon 6/7 series): Meaningfully faster, better power efficiency, handle gaming and multitasking well. Found in MWK 150k–400k phones.
  • Flagship chips (Snapdragon 8 series, MediaTek Dimensity 9000+): Top-tier performance, mainly relevant if you edit video or play GPU-heavy games. Found in MWK 500k+ phones.

4. Storage: Harder to Expand Than It Looks

Some phones accept microSD cards; many newer ones do not. If you are choosing between 64GB and 128GB internal storage at a small price difference, 128GB is almost always the right answer. WhatsApp media alone — voice notes, photos, and videos in Malawian family and work groups — can consume 10–15GB in a few months without deliberate management. 64GB fills up and starts causing problems before most buyers expect it to.

If microSD expansion is important to you, verify the phone has a dedicated microSD slot before purchasing. Some phones use a hybrid SIM/microSD tray that forces a choice between a second SIM and storage expansion — check this if you use dual SIMs, which many Malawians do for TNM and Airtel.

5. Look for Real Battery Life, Not Just Capacity

Manufacturer battery claims are measured under controlled conditions that do not reflect Malawian daily use — full mobile data coverage, constant messaging, and long stretches without wall socket access due to ESCOM load shedding. Look for independent reviews that report screen-on time under normal conditions. A 5,000mAh phone with efficient software can outlast a 6,000mAh phone with poor software optimisation.

Also compare: does the phone support fast charging? What wattage? A phone that goes from 20% to 80% in 40 minutes is far more practical in Malawi during short power windows than one that takes two and a half hours on a basic 10W charger.

6. Camera: Test It, Do Not Trust the Spec Sheet

The quickest useful comparison is looking at sample photos taken by real users — ideally in the same conditions you shoot in. Indoor photos in low light. Outdoor daytime. Front camera for calls and social media. The gap between a 50MP camera with average processing and a 12MP camera with excellent processing is usually obvious from sample photos, and the spec sheet tells you nothing about it.

7. Software Update Commitment

Samsung commits to four OS updates and five years of security patches. Google Pixel matches this. Xiaomi and Motorola commit to three years. Tecno and itel update less consistently. A phone that stops receiving security updates within a year is increasingly at risk from known vulnerabilities. For a phone you plan to use for three or four years, this matters more than most buyers think at the time of purchase.

8. Used vs New at the Same Price

A used flagship and a new mid-ranger at the same price often compare well on specs but diverge on three things: battery health, screen condition, and remaining update support. A used Samsung S21 at MWK 350,000 has specs that look better than a new Samsung A35 at MWK 350,000. But the S21 may have two years of update support remaining, a battery at 78% health, and a screen that may or may not be original. The A35 has four years of updates ahead, full battery health, and a warranty. The comparison is not the spec sheet — it is the total picture.
