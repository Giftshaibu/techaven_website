How to Choose the Right Smartphone Before Buying

Choose based on how you actually use your phone — not what gets the most attention online.

Back to blog
By Techaven Editorial Team Published 25 January 2026 Updated 2 May 2026

The right smartphone depends on how you actually use your phone — not which model gets the most attention on YouTube reviews or social media. Malawian buyers face a specific set of priorities that do not always align with what global tech reviewers emphasise: reliable battery performance under load shedding, network compatibility with TNM and Airtel, durability for daily transport by minibus, and genuine value within a limited budget.

This guide walks through each decision point in order of importance for most buyers in Malawi. Use it before you start browsing listings on Techaven.

Step 1: Set Your Budget Before You Look at Anything

The gap between what you want to spend and what looks appealing when you start comparing is where most buyers get into trouble. Decide the number before you open a single listing. Rough budget categories in Malawi's 2026 market:

  • Under MWK 100,000: Entry-level Android (Tecno Spark, itel series). Handles calls, WhatsApp, and mobile money. Camera and performance are basic.
  • MWK 100,000–250,000: Budget-to-mid-range Android (Tecno Camon, Samsung Galaxy A15/A25, Xiaomi Redmi series). Solid everyday performance.
  • MWK 250,000–500,000: Mid-range Android (Samsung Galaxy A35/A55, Xiaomi Redmi Note 13 Pro). Noticeably better cameras, processors, and build quality.
  • MWK 500,000+: Upper mid-range and flagship (Samsung Galaxy S-series, iPhone 13 and above, OnePlus). Significant performance leap but diminishing returns for typical Malawian use cases.

Step 2: Think Honestly About Your Use Cases

List what you actually do on your phone for an entire week. For most Malawians, the list is: WhatsApp, voice calls, Airtel Money or TNM Mpamba transactions, social media browsing, basic photography, music, and occasionally mobile data for browsing. A mid-range Android handles all of this without strain. You do not need flagship specs for that workload.

Where you will feel the difference is in heavier use: video editing, running multiple business apps simultaneously, GPU-heavy games like PUBG Mobile, or streaming high-definition content over mobile data. If these are regular activities, a budget device will frustrate you within weeks.

Step 3: RAM and Storage Are More Important Than Brand Marketing

4GB RAM is the practical minimum for smooth Android performance in 2026. 6GB or 8GB makes a noticeable difference if you keep many apps open — which most people do, given background apps for mobile banking, WhatsApp, and social media. For storage, 64GB fills up faster than expected once you account for photos, WhatsApp media, and apps. 128GB is the comfortable starting point if your budget allows it.

Some phones support microSD card expansion. If storage matters to you and the phone supports a memory card, this gives you flexibility. Check before buying — not all mid-range phones include the slot.

Step 4: Battery Capacity Is Critical in Malawi

For Malawian users who experience ESCOM load shedding across Lilongwe, Blantyre, Mzuzu, and secondary towns, battery performance is often more important than any other spec. A 5,000mAh battery with decent software optimisation will outlast a 4,000mAh battery with poor software management — look at independent reviews that test real-world screen-on time, not manufacturer claims.

Also check whether the phone supports fast charging and whether the original charger included (or the one you will buy) matches the phone's spec. A phone with 33W fast charging is much more practical during short power windows than one that takes three hours on a 5W charger.

Step 5: Camera — Real Photos Beat Megapixel Numbers

A 50MP sensor with weak image processing produces worse photos than a well-tuned 12MP sensor. The only reliable comparison is looking at sample photos from actual users in similar lighting conditions to what you shoot in. Night photography is where budget phones diverge most from mid-range and above — if you take photos in the evening or indoors, this matters.

Step 6: Software Update Commitment

Some brands commit to three to four years of Android OS and security updates. Others stop after one. A phone that stops receiving security updates within a year becomes a growing security risk as vulnerabilities go unpatched. Samsung and Google commit to the longest update windows. Xiaomi and Motorola are reasonable. Tecno and itel update less consistently — fine for budget use but worth knowing.

Step 7: Used vs New at the Same Price

A used flagship with a replaced screen and degraded battery is not the same as a new mid-ranger at the same price, even if the spec sheet looks better. When comparing used vs new, adjust for actual condition. A used phone with 75% battery health and an aftermarket screen is competing at a discount from its stated spec, not at face value.

Frequently Asked Questions

4GB gets you through WhatsApp, calls, social media, and mobile money without trouble. Start keeping lots of apps open at once and you'll feel it. 6GB or 8GB makes that less of an issue.

Samsung and Tecno have the best parts availability in Lilongwe and Blantyre. iPhones can be repaired but parts cost more and take longer. Xiaomi and itel are fine in the cities, but harder to fix outside them.

Most unlocked Android phones work on both TNM and Airtel 4G. Officially sold iPhones do too. Where it gets tricky is phones imported from other regions — they may not support all local LTE bands. Check the specs against Band 3, Band 20, and Band 28 before buying one of those.

For everyday use, mid-range gets you most of what a flagship does at half the price. The gap is real if you edit video, game seriously, or need the best camera. For everything else, mid-range is just the better deal.
